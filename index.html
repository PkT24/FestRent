— Launch Pipeline
01
Build the Platform Core
Month 1–2

No users yet. Build the procurement infrastructure that makes FestRent different from emailing suppliers manually — the bid request system, supplier verification, and contract layer.

Build organizer-side: event posting, equipment needs specification, bid review dashboard
Build supplier-side: profile creation, verified credentials, bid submission with pricing and availability
Design supplier verification pipeline — business registration, insurance proof, equipment portfolio
Build contract generation — auto-drafted agreements based on accepted bid terms, signed in-platform
Integrate escrow payments — organizer pays on contract acceptance, supplier paid on delivery confirmation
02
Land 3 Organizer Discovery Calls
Month 1 (parallel)

Before writing more code, validate that procurement pain is real. Find 3 GTA event organizers and ask them how they currently source equipment. Their answers will reshape everything.

Target: community festival committees, college event boards, cultural fair organizers — approachable decision-makers
Cold outreach on LinkedIn — short, direct ask for 20 minutes, no pitch yet
Ask: "How do you currently source equipment?" / "What's the worst part of that process?" / "Have you ever had a supplier fall through?"
Listen for: time spent sourcing, fallthrough rates, price surprises, contract disputes — these are your product requirements
03
Onboard Verified Suppliers
Month 2–3

Use a confirmed organizer interest as your recruitment pitch to suppliers. You're not asking them to join a platform — you're telling them there's a specific event contract available to bid on.

Target small-to-mid equipment rental firms, AV companies, tent & furniture suppliers across GTA
Also recruit serious individual owners — someone with a cargo van, 50 chairs, and a weekend free is a real supplier
Run each supplier through full verification before approval — insurance, references, equipment photos
Target 15–25 verified suppliers across core categories before first organizer goes live
04
Run the First Live Procurement
Month 3–4

Get one real organizer to post a real equipment need and let verified suppliers bid on it. Manage this one manually if needed — your job is to learn what breaks before automating it.

Work with the organizer to spec their needs clearly — quantities, dates, delivery requirements, budget range
Notify matched suppliers and guide them through the bid process hands-on
Facilitate contract signing and escrow payment — handle edge cases manually, document everything
Be on the ground day-of — confirm delivery, handle disputes, collect testimonials from both sides
Track: bid response rate, time-to-contract, organizer satisfaction, supplier friction points
05
Scale the Marketplace
Month 5+

Use the first contract as proof. Expand organizer base, deepen supplier network, and automate the coordination layer. Start exploring multi-category procurement and repeat organizer deals.

Build repeatable onboarding playbook for organizers — templated need specs by event type
Approach larger events armed with transaction proof — completed contracts, on-time delivery rate, testimonials
Introduce preferred supplier tier — higher-rated suppliers get early bid access and lower commission
Expand into multi-vendor procurement — one organizer, multiple simultaneous bid categories
Explore annual retainer model for repeat organizers running multiple events per year

— Key Risks & Mitigations
🐔
Chicken & Egg Problem
Organizers won't post needs without suppliers. Suppliers won't onboard without active procurement requests.
→ Manually seed supply first. Recruit 15–25 verified suppliers before the first organizer goes live — so day-one bids come in fast.
📋
Supplier Fallthrough
Accepted supplier doesn't deliver. Organizer has 500 people showing up and no chairs. Platform credibility destroyed instantly.
→ Escrow held until delivery confirmed. Penalty clauses in auto-generated contracts. Backup supplier list maintained for pilot events.
✂️
Disintermediation
Organizer takes a supplier's number after the first contract and cuts FestRent out of future deals.
→ Own the contract layer, payment history, and ratings. The platform value compounds — switching means losing verified history and escrow protection.
